"the Cry" Artworks

A controversy that had spanned for over a century

The Monuments

The Cry of Balintawak Monument

There are a multitude of art and figures made by prominent artists to honor the start of the Katipuneros Revolution. Possibly the most important piece of art to be discussed in relation to “the Cry” is the Cry of Balintawak Monument inaugurated on Sept. 3, 1911, being considered the first national memorial for Bonifacio and the Katipunan (Samonte, 2018). By 1968, the Cry of Balintawak Monument was transferred to the University of the Philippines Diliman. This transfer of the monument caused a large amount of confusion on the details of “the Cry” as according to Borromeo-Buehler (1998), the monument’s original inscription of “A los heroes del 96" and two tablets with the dates 26 August 1896 and 3 September 1911 were removed and replaced with a “Cry of Pugad Lawin” inscription (p. 8). It is noteworthy that due to the nature of the transferring of the monument and the replacing of the original inscriptions on the monument, the evidence points that the first monument for “the Cry” was in fact associated with Balintawak.

Andres Bonifacio Monument

After the Cry of Balintawak Monument came the Andres Bonifacio monument inaugurated on October 23, 1933 which commemorates Andres Bonifacio together with his Katipuneros depicts and recalls the call of arms of the Katipunan to revolt against the Spaniard Revolution in 1896 (Official Gazette PH, n.d.).

Paintings

The Bonifacio Mural

Another noteworthy work of art is potentially the first painting to depict “the Cry”, which is Carlo Francisco’s Bonifacio Mural. Carlo Francisco’s great painting, similar to the other artworks discussed, is a depiction of Andres Bonifacio and his fellow Katipuneros in the beginning of the Philippine revolution against Spain in 1986. According to the Philippine Folklife Museum (2014), the Bonifacio Mural is also known as the Cry of Balintawak or Cry of Pugad Lawin. It is worthy to note that on the Philippine Folklife Museum page for the Bonifacio Mural, the article is entitled "The Cry of Balintawak", which may allude more to the part that “the Cry” may have resulted in Balintawak.

The Shrine of Pugad Lawin

The Pugad Lawin Shrine located in Barangay Bahay Toro, Project 8, Quezon City is a historical site that pays tribute to the “Cry.” On the site is a commemorative marker bearing the inscription (in Filipino):

Ang Sigaw ng Pugad Lawin (1896)

Sa paligid ng pook na ito, si Andres Bonifacio at mga isang libong Katipunero ay nagpulong noong umaga ng ika-23 Agosto 1896, at ipinasyang maghimagsik laban sa pamahalaang Kastila sa Pilipinas. Pinagpunit-punit ang kanilang mga sedula na naging tanda ng pagkaalipin ng mga Pilipino. Ito ang kauna-unahang sigaw ng bayang apu laban sa bansang Espanya na pinatibayan sa pamamagitan ng paggamit ng sandata.

Found in the site as well are bronze figures of twenty-five Katipuneros by Napoleon Abueva. Included here are the well-known members of the association namely, Andres Bonifacio, Melchora Aquino de Ramos or Tandang Sora, Emilio Jacinto, Pio Valenzuela, Teodoro Plata, Aguedo del Rosario, Briccio B. Pantas, Procopio de Castro Bonifacio, Apolonio Samson, Ramon Bernardo, Alejandro Santiago, and Juan Ramos (Olivares, 2020).

The shrine’s marker by the National Historical Commission of the Philippines (NHCP) is mainly based on the accounts of Pio Valenzuela, the Vice-President of the Katipunan during the revolution, who claimed that the “Cry” took place in the residence of Juan A. Ramos, Tandang Sora’s son, in “Pugad Lawin” where the shrine stands today. In the study of the history of the “Cry,” it was Filipino historian Teodoro A. Agoncillo who adapted most of Valenzuela’s narrative to deduce the specific details of this historic event mainly basing on the former Katipunero’s “Memoirs” from the 1920s. Both fueled by the narration of Valenzuela, the shrine’s proclamation mirrors Agoncillo’s interpretation suggesting that the pagpupunit transpired after the pasya.

Nonetheless, according to a research by Richardson (2019), Dr. Valenzuela left a trace of inconsistencies in his narration of the “Cry,” particularly in terms of its location. Starting from his initial statement in 1911, he said that the decision to revolt was made in Apolonio Samson’s house in Kangkong which he later changed to Tandang Sora’s house in Pasong Tamo based on his testimony in 1917. It was only then in his 1920s “Memoirs” as noted by Agoncillo, and the joint statement he issued with Briccio Pantas and Cipriano Pacheco, that he finalized his claim saying that the “Cry” did not transpire in Balintawak but in “the place known by the name of Pugad Lawin.”

Further analysis also suggest that “Pugad Lawin” could not be considered as the location of the “Cry” based on the following reasons. First, the revolt was started by the people of Balintawak against the Spaniards, which is why the name “Cry of Pugad Lawin'' is deemed inappropriate. Second, the town of Pugad Lawin was founded in 1935, following the 1896 uprising. Finally, the phrase “Pugad Lawin” was coined as a result of a hawk’s nest in the backyard of Tandang Sora in Banlat, Gulod, Kaloocan, which is supposed to have been one of the hiding spots of Andres Bonifacio's revolutionary group.

As a matter of fact, the “Cry” can also be linked to some accounts that connect directly to Balintawak. The event took place in Balintawak, according to Lt. Olegario Diaz of the Spanish Civil Guards, who corroborated the stories of historians Gregorio Zaide and Teodoro Kalaw. Interestingly as well, the work of Teodoro Agoncillo himself called the Revolt of the Masses which is considered to be “the standard work on the Katipunan” that relies mostly on Pio Valenzuela’s testimony of the “Cry,” also indicated sources from other KKK veterans namely Guillermo Masangkay and Francisco Carreon who both did not acknowledge the existence of a place called “Pugad Lawin” at that time (Richardson, 2019).
